\begin{abstract}
Reliable early screening of breast cancer requires capturing complementary anatomical and physiological information; however, no single imaging modality can simultaneously provide comprehensive characterization. Mammography remains the standard for structural detail, yet its sensitivity drops in dense glandular tissue and its use of ionizing radiation is a documented concern. Thermography captures vascular and metabolic asymmetry without any radiation exposure but it lacks specificity for downstream clinical action. The proposed pipeline fuses features from the two streams at the latent representation level instead of raw pixel level. Mammograms flow through a pretrained ResNet50 backbone model. Thermograms flow through a lightweight MobileNetV2 model. Each embedding is projected into a shared 128 dimensional latent space and aligned using a mean squared error objective, and the aligned representations are concatenated for downstream inference. Minimum Redundancy Maximum Relevance (mRMR) selection trims the fused vector before a dense softmax head produces the binary call between benign and malignant. Training uses the CBIS\textendash DDSM mammography dataset and the Visual DMR\textendash IR thermography dataset with 70/15/15 percent train, validation and test splits and preserved class balance. On the held out test set the pipeline reaches 94.5 percent classification accuracy and a Precision Recall Area Under Curve of 0.991. Under Gaussian noise perturbation accuracy only slips to 93.1 percent. Two dimensional t\textendash distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t\textendash SNE) and Principal Component Analysis (PCA) projections show class separation. SHAP based post hoc analysis then flags the small subset of latent dimensions doing most of the discriminative work.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Breast cancer remains among the leading causes of mortality among women worldwide. Over 2.3 million new cases are reported each year \cite{sung2021global}. Mammography is still the current gold standard for screening \cite{michell2018role, yala2022deep}, but the diagnostic performance is bounded by several factors simultaneously: breast tissue density, exposure to ionizing radiation, and inter observer variability across radiologists. This has driven a growing interest in adjunct imaging modalities \cite{ng2022review, arora2008effectiveness}. Infrared thermography stands out among these. No ionizing dose, and the acquisition procedure itself is rapid and painless. The physiological insight is complementary rather than duplicative of what mammography already captures.

Deep learning has shifted the performance ceiling in both mammogram and thermogram analysis, and not incrementally so. Convolutional networks are now capable of detecting lesions, microcalcification clusters and vascular asymmetries that a radiologist can genuinely miss on unaided visual inspection, particularly at the earliest disease stage where the visible signal is faint or occluded within dense glandular tissue. Numbers back this up. Recent mammography benchmarks report classification accuracies above 85 percent \cite{Shen2019DeepLearningMammo, Ragab2019CBISDDSM, AlMnayyis2025}. Thermography deep models report performance within a comparable range \cite{pereira2020infrared, Roslidar2020, Alzahrani2025}. Throughput matters too, arguably more than the accuracy gap given how screening workflows actually function in a hospital setting. A deep model handles thousands of screening cases in the interval a human reader requires for a hundred, and the diagnostic value derives from recovering malignancy patterns embedded in pixel statistics rather than in features the human visual system was ever trained to detect.

Thermography reads temperature asymmetries and vascular heat signatures, and these reflect the underlying metabolic and angiogenic changes that come with malignancy \cite{head1979infrared, ng2009breast}. Machine learning has changed the practical calculus here. Automated feature extraction now recovers thermal patterns that no hand crafted feature would reliably pick up \cite{pereira2020infrared, el2021machine}. But thermography alone is often not specific enough to separate benign heat anomalies from malignant ones. That is the main practical reason thermography has not replaced structural imaging as a standalone screening modality \cite{vishnu2023review}.

Mammography, in contrast, gives high resolution anatomical and structural detail, and it picks up microcalcifications and soft tissue densities that thermography simply cannot visualize. Mammographic interpretation nevertheless suffers in dense breasts, where the surrounding fibroglandular tissue can obscure lesions and drive false negatives with reduced sensitivity in younger populations \cite{boyd2007mammographic, destounis2021supplemental}. Those complementary strengths and weaknesses are the underlying reason to look at multimodal fusion of thermography and mammography in the first place \cite{mostafa2022multi, khan2023hybrid}.

Clinical adoption of any such model needs one more thing on top of raw accuracy. Explainability of individual decisions produced by the pipeline. Radiologists will not, and arguably should not, trust a black box that emits a probability score with nothing attached to it. Post hoc tools like SHAP matter here. They identify which features or which scan regions actually drove a particular prediction and that is what allows informed clinical disagreement with the model and the sort of regulatory audit trail any real deployment ends up needing.

The present study develops a multimodal analysis framework for early breast cancer detection. Thermographic input comes from the Visual DMR\textendash IR dataset \cite{Moreira2014VisualDMR, Silva2018DMRIR}. Quadrant wise segmentation plus temperature normalisation captures localised vascular and metabolic patterns on that side. Mammographic input comes from CBIS\textendash DDSM \cite{Lee2017CBISDDSM}, with structural features extracted through deep convolutional encoders on the other side. Both modality streams are then mapped into a shared latent representation. The joint discriminative behaviour is examined next in order to understand how structural and physiological imaging complement each other for breast abnormality classification.

The objective of the present paper is deliberately narrow. It does not propose a clinical computer aided diagnosis system ready for direct deployment. The study instead examines the synergistic diagnostic value of combining these two modalities within the specific setting of thermal biology. The experiments show that thermographic imagery does carry biologically relevant temperature signatures. Those signatures complement structural mammographic features once the two representations are joined at the latent level rather than fused at the raw pixel level.

\subsection{Related Work}

Recent advances in breast cancer imaging cluster around three lines: mammography, thermography and multimodal fusion. Work published in the 2024 to 2025 window has particularly emphasised validation across diverse datasets and complementary imaging modalities.

On the mammography side, Shen et al. \cite{Shen2019DeepLearningMammo} trained a deep network end to end on CBIS\textendash DDSM for the binary benign versus malignant classification task, reporting around 86 percent accuracy in what was one of the earliest reliable numbers on this corpus. Ragab et al. \cite{Ragab2019CBISDDSM} combined a CNN feature extractor with a Support Vector Machine head, again on CBIS\textendash DDSM, and reported around 87 percent for the same binary label set (benign versus malignant). Al\textendash Mnayyis et al. \cite{AlMnayyis2025} developed a CNN classifier for mammographic cancer detection using the KAUH\textendash BCMD dataset, which contains 7{,}205 images from over 5{,}000 patients, and they reported consistent accuracy around 96 percent for the binary benign versus malignant classes across heterogeneous data, with the blunt message that large, clinically diverse validation sets matter more than architectural tweaks. Chen et al. \cite{Chen2025multimodal} extended this line by integrating mammography with ultrasound for lesion classification (binary: benign or malignant), reporting around 91.5 percent test accuracy.

In the thermography domain, Jalloul et al. \cite{Jalloul2024} compared classical and deep learning approaches for infrared breast imaging and reported classification accuracies of 94 to 98 percent using optimized feature extraction together with Support Vector Machines on a binary benign versus malignant label set, and their result confirmed that thermal imaging does encode diagnostically useful physiological information. Alzahrani et al. \cite{Alzahrani2025} built on that direction by designing an end to end CNN enhanced with particle swarm optimization to classify malignant versus benign thermograms, reaching nearly 98 percent accuracy and further demonstrating the viability of thermal signatures once the learning architecture is optimized carefully. Roslidar et al. \cite{Roslidar2020} surveyed deep learning approaches applied to DMR\textendash IR thermograms and reported a wide accuracy range of 85 to 97 percent, with binary settings (benign versus malignant) consistently easier than three class settings (healthy, benign, malignant). The collective conclusion from these studies is that deep learning is a good fit for thermographic breast analysis. This despite the datasets remaining small in absolute terms.

Parallel to the unimodal literature, several studies have pursued multimodal fusion in order to combine structural and physiological imaging within a single decision pipeline. Wei et al. \cite{Wei2025} proposed a multimodal framework that aligned ultrasound views with radiology text reports through dual encoders and cross modal feature alignment, reporting around 90 percent binary classification accuracy. Li et al. \cite{Li2025review} provided a systematic review of fusion methodologies and summarized feature level, decision level and hybrid multimodal fusion strategies in a single framework. Hou \cite{Hou2025} carried out a bibliometric analysis of AI assisted multimodal breast imaging and reported a marked research shift toward integrating MRI, mammography and thermography together rather than in isolation. Mostafa and Hassanien \cite{mostafa2022multi} experimented specifically with feature level fusion of thermographic and mammographic streams and reported around 92 percent accuracy for the binary benign versus malignant setting, while Khan et al. \cite{khan2023hybrid} adopted a hybrid deep learning approach with thermography plus mammography and reported around 94 percent binary accuracy on a mixed cohort.

Read as a whole this body of work says a few things. Recent mammography efforts have been pushing accuracy upward through larger corpora and cross center validation regimes and thermographic deep models have separately established that heat pattern signals carry real diagnostic information rather than being incidental artefacts of camera placement. But large chunks of the literature stay unimodal, or fuse modalities without an interpretability layer to explain what the fusion actually did, or draw their conclusions from cohorts too narrow to speak to real deployment. The multimodal shift is happening. It is not finished. The present paper positions itself within that shift, taking thermal and mammographic modalities and joining them at the latent representation level so that the resulting classifier can be interrogated rather than trusted on faith.



\section{Methods}

The methodology combines mammographic and thermographic imagery within a single learning pipeline. ResNet50 handles the mammographic feature extraction. MobileNetV2 handles the thermogram side. Downstream of these two backbones everything is standardised, comprising normalisation, fusion, dimensionality reduction and a post hoc explainability layer positioned on the classifier output. In code the pipeline runs as three sequential passes over each subject. Preprocessing and normalisation come first, per modality. Then deep feature extraction through the two backbones. Then multimodal fusion of the resulting feature streams and downstream binary classification over the joint representation.

\subsubsection{Mammography Dataset: CBIS\textendash DDSM}

The mammographic data are derived from the Curated Breast Imaging Subset of the Digital Database for Screening Mammography (CBIS\textendash DDSM) \cite{Lee2017CBISDDSM}. This dataset is an updated and standardized version of the original DDSM repository, curated by the Cancer Imaging Archive (TCIA). It contains 2{,}620 ROI images extracted from digitized film mammograms encompassing approximately 1{,}500 patients. Each image is annotated with:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Lesion type: \textit{mass, calcification}
    \item Pathology: \textit{benign, malignant}
    \item Breast density (BI\textendash RADS I to IV)
    \item Bounding boxes and pixel level lesion masks
\end{itemize}
All mammograms were converted from 16 bit grayscale to 8 bit, normalized to the range $[0,1]$, and resized to $224\times224$ pixels. The images were then reshaped into 3D tensors of size $224\times224\times3$ by channel replication so that they became compatible with convolutional backbones.

\subsubsection{Thermography Dataset: Visual DMR\textendash IR}

The thermographic data were obtained from the Visual DMR\textendash IR dataset \cite{Moreira2014VisualDMR, Silva2018DMRIR}. It consists of 200 participants imaged under standardized ambient conditions (20 to 22 degrees Celsius, 40 to 60 percent humidity). Each subject was imaged using a FLIR A320 infrared camera capturing frontal and oblique views of both breasts. Each thermogram is a temperature matrix $T(x,y)$ corresponding to surface thermal emission in Kelvin. Each is categorized as \textit{healthy}, \textit{benign}, or \textit{malignant} based on biopsy confirmed outcomes. Representative samples from both imaging modalities are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:dataset_samples}. Thermal distribution patterns in the thermographic panels differ between classes. Mammographic panels show the corresponding structural variation associated with benign and malignant breast abnormalities.

\paragraph{Sampling rationale} A note on data volume is warranted. Both datasets contain more raw material than the present study actually consumes. CBIS\textendash DDSM offers thousands of full mammograms along with their ROI crops. A balanced subset of 2{,}620 ROI images was sampled to correspond approximately to what the DMR\textendash IR side could support and to keep the two class distributions comparable across modalities. On the thermography side, DMR\textendash IR is recorded from 200 subjects. Frontal and oblique frames from a subset of these were sampled to preserve acquisition geometry consistency. The rationale for sampling rather than using every available image is twofold. Class balance across the two datasets is a prerequisite, since otherwise the latent alignment step would learn a spurious modality prior. Furthermore, using the full corpora at their native size would have shifted the emphasis of the study away from the fusion mechanism itself, which is the actual object of investigation of this paper.

The two datasets come from independent cohorts. CBIS\textendash DDSM and Visual DMR\textendash IR were not acquired on the same patients. Alignment is therefore done at the class level, keyed off diagnostic category labels rather than any patient wise correspondence. Splits into train, validation and test partitions were carried out independently within each modality at a 70 / 15 / 15 percent ratio, with the class distribution preserved on each side. The binary setup collapses DMR\textendash IR healthy and benign cases into a single benign class. Both modalities then carry the same two label semantics: benign versus malignant.

\subsection{Preprocessing Pipeline}
Fig.~\ref{fig:framework} shows the end to end workflow of the proposed thermo mammographic pipeline running through preprocessing, feature extraction, latent alignment, fusion, classification and the SHAP explainability layer stacked on top.
The preprocessing stage does two jobs at once. Radiometric consistency within each modality. Preparation of the tensors for downstream deep feature embedding. For a given mammogram $I_m(x,y)$ and thermogram $I_t(x,y)$ the overall transformation reads:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{P} = \{P_M(I_m), P_T(I_t)\} \rightarrow \{I_m', I_t'\}
\label{eq:preproc_set}
\end{equation}
where $P_M$ and $P_T$ denote the preprocessing operators applied to mammography and thermography respectively.

\subsubsection{Mammogram Preprocessing (ResNet50 Pipeline)}

The mammographic routine starts from raw DICOM or digital mammogram inputs. These are decoded and cast to 8 bit grayscale arrays. Subsequent steps then run in the following fixed order:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Histogram Equalization:} The grayscale image is contrast normalized using adaptive histogram equalization to enhance lesion boundaries and glandular tissue visibility \cite{Pizer1987CLAHE}.
    \item \textbf{Standardization:} Pixel intensities are normalized to zero mean and unit variance so that acquisition bias is removed:
    \begin{equation}
    I_m'(x,y) = \frac{I_m(x,y) - \mu_m}{\sigma_m}
    \label{eq:mammo_norm_code}
    \end{equation}
    where $\mu_m$ and $\sigma_m$ denote the mean and standard deviation of pixel intensities respectively.
    \item \textbf{Dynamic Range Scaling:} Normalized values are linearly rescaled to $[0,255]$ to preserve the image's dynamic range:
    \begin{equation}
    I_m''(x,y) = 255 \times \frac{I_m'(x,y) - \min(I_m')}{\max(I_m') - \min(I_m')}
    \label{eq:mammo_rescale}
    \end{equation}
    \item \textbf{Resizing and RGB Expansion:} The mammograms are resized to $224\times224$ and replicated across three channels to form $I_m''' \in \mathbb{R}^{224\times224\times3}$. The channel replication is a hard requirement of the ResNet50 input signature. Downstream CNN stacks expect three colour channels even though the mammogram itself is single channel \cite{He2016ResNet}.
\end{enumerate}

The processed image is then handed to the ResNet50 backbone for deep feature embedding. The pretrained convolutional stack picks up the hierarchical structural patterns from there.

\subsubsection{Thermogram Preprocessing (MobileNetV2 Pipeline)}

Thermal imaging needs specialized handling because of varying emissivity and thermal contrast. The preprocessing routine here pins the temperature scaling down and knocks out sensor noise before anything goes into the deep encoder.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Input Normalization:} Raw thermal maps are converted from absolute temperature matrices to 8 bit grayscale intensity maps. Normalization is performed as:
    \begin{equation}
    T'(x,y) = \frac{T(x,y) - \min(T)}{\max(T) - \min(T)} \times 255
    \label{eq:temp_norm_code}
    \end{equation}
    This transformation puts pixel intensities on a uniform scale across subjects.
    \item \textbf{Noise Suppression:} Gaussian blur with kernel size $(3\times3)$ removes sensor noise and thermal inconsistencies without degrading vascular features.
    \item \textbf{Segmentation:} A binary mask is generated to isolate the breast region based on intensity thresholding:
    \begin{equation}
    M_t(x,y) =
    \begin{cases}
    1, & \text{if } T'(x,y) > \theta \\
    0, & \text{otherwise}
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:thermo_mask}
    \end{equation}
    where $\theta$ denotes a fixed temperature based threshold.
    \item \textbf{Resizing and Channel Duplication:} Each thermogram is resized to $224\times224$ pixels and duplicated across three channels. MobileNetV2 was pretrained on RGB natural images and expects the same three channel input shape at inference, so single channel thermal data has to be tiled up to match \cite{Sandler2018MobileNetV2}.
\end{enumerate}

Both preprocessing pipelines leave the mammographic and thermographic tensors normalised. Spatially consistent. Radiometrically calibrated. That is the input contract the downstream deep feature extraction stage assumes.

Data augmentation was applied during training to keep the model from overfitting the modest cohort size. The transforms used were the standard set for medical imagery random horizontal flipping, rotations bounded to $\pm 10^\circ$, small zoom perturbations and slight intensity jitter. Nothing exotic. The point is not to fabricate anatomy but to simulate the small variations in patient positioning, camera calibration and exposure that a real deployment pipeline would see, so that the classifier stops keying on artefacts that happen to be constant across the training set.

\subsection{Feature Extraction Framework}

Feature extraction runs through convolutional backbones pretrained on ImageNet and then fine tuned for the domain. Mammography and thermography branches run independently up until the fusion step.

\paragraph{Why ResNet50 for mammography and MobileNetV2 for thermography.} The two backbones were not chosen at random. Mammograms carry rich high frequency structural information: microcalcification clusters, spiculations, tissue density gradients. Residual connections in ResNet50 let very deep filter stacks propagate gradients cleanly, which is exactly what one wants for these fine grained structural cues. The 2048 dimensional embedding it produces is expressive enough to encode subtle structural anomalies without collapsing them into a shorter code. Thermograms, on the other hand, are lower in spatial detail, noisier at the sensor level, and the DMR\textendash IR corpus is much smaller than a typical natural image dataset. Deep heavy backbones tend to overfit here. MobileNetV2, with its inverted residuals and depthwise separable convolutions, is a much better fit in this regime. Its compact 1280 dimensional embedding is well suited to smaller data volumes and captures the coarser vascular and metabolic asymmetries that the thermal signal actually carries. In short: ResNet50 gives us depth where depth pays off; MobileNetV2 gives us compactness where compactness is a defence against overfitting.

\subsubsection{Mammographic Feature Encoding}

Each preprocessed mammogram $I_m'''$ is fed into the ResNet50 convolutional backbone \cite{He2016ResNet}. The final fully connected layer is removed and global average pooling is applied, giving a compact high level feature representation:
\begin{equation}
f_M = \phi_M(I_m'''; \theta_M)
\label{eq:resnet}
\end{equation}
where $\phi_M$ represents the feature extraction function and $\theta_M$ are the pretrained parameters. The resulting feature vector $f_M \in \mathbb{R}^{2048}$ carries several kinds of information at once. Tissue density gradients. Microcalcification texture. Spiculated margins around lesions. These are the visual cues radiologists themselves use for the benign versus malignant call, now folded into a compact numerical representation.

\subsubsection{Thermographic Feature Encoding}

The thermographic branch is handled by MobileNetV2. That backbone was picked because it stays lightweight under limited data and does not blow up in parameter count relative to the size of DMR\textendash IR \cite{Sandler2018MobileNetV2}. Each normalized thermogram $I_t'$ is passed through MobileNetV2 as:
\begin{equation}
f_T = \phi_T(I_t'; \theta_T)
\label{eq:mobilenet}
\end{equation}
where $\phi_T$ represents the thermographic encoder and $\theta_T$ are pretrained weights. The extracted features $f_T \in \mathbb{R}^{1280}$ pick up a different family of signals altogether. Superficial vascular topology. Local metabolic hot spots. Left right asymmetries in surface temperature. These are the physiologically driven cues that a malignant lesion typically leaves on the skin surface even before it is visible on a mammogram.

\subsection{Multimodal Feature Fusion and Classification}

Once modality specific feature vectors $f_M$ and $f_T$ are in hand they are concatenated at the feature level to form a unified multimodal representation:
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}_{fusion} = [f_M \Vert f_T]
\label{eq:concat}
\end{equation}
The concatenation performs two roles simultaneously. Mammography contributes the structural side of the representation, including calcifications, spiculated margins and tissue density gradients, while thermography contributes the vascular and metabolic aspects of the tumour environment, and the two feature streams reside alongside each other within the joint vector. Neither modality on its own carries the complete diagnostic picture. In combination they do \cite{Khan2023Hybrid}.

Concatenated deep representations tend to introduce feature redundancy and correlated latent activations though, especially in multimodal settings. So dimensionality reduction is performed using the Minimum Redundancy Maximum Relevance (mRMR) criterion \cite{Peng2005mRMR}:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{S}^* = \arg\max_{\mathcal{S}} \left[ \frac{1}{|\mathcal{S}|}\sum_{x_i \in \mathcal{S}} I(x_i; c) - \frac{1}{|\mathcal{S}|^2}\sum_{x_i, x_j \in \mathcal{S}} I(x_i; x_j) \right]
\label{eq:mrmr_code}
\end{equation}
where $I(\cdot)$ denotes mutual information and $c$ is the class label. The reduced feature subset $\mathcal{S}^*$ is then fed into a fully connected neural classifier:
\begin{equation}
\hat{y} = \mathrm{Softmax}(W_c \mathbf{F}_{fusion} + b_c)
\label{eq:classifier_code}
\end{equation}
The classifier predicts the probability of malignancy using categorical cross entropy loss:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{k=1}^{C}y_{ik}\log(\hat{y}_{ik})
\label{eq:loss}
\end{equation}
where $y_{ik}$ and $\hat{y}_{ik}$ represent ground truth and predicted probabilities for class $k$ respectively.

\subsection{Explainability with SHAP}
\vspace{-0.2em}

Post hoc interpretability is achieved using SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) \cite{Lundberg2017SHAP}. The core idea is game theoretic. Every input feature is treated as a player and its Shapley value quantifies how much that player pulls the model output away from a baseline. The classifier decision is thus written as an additive decomposition over per feature contributions:
\begin{equation}
f(\mathbf{x}) = f(\mathbf{x'}) + \sum_{i=1}^{n}\phi_i
\label{eq:shap_code}
\end{equation}
where $\phi_i$ represents the marginal contribution of feature $x_i$ to the prediction. SHAP values were computed for both modalities and mapped so the individual features (lesion asymmetry, temperature gradient and so on) could be tied back to the malignancy prediction they actually pushed. Decisions become inspectable at the feature level. That inspection surface is what medical decision support use requires in the first place.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Multimodal Feature Extraction and Fusion for Breast Cancer Classification}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require $\mathcal{D}_M = \{I_m^{(i)}, y^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^{N}$, $\mathcal{D}_T = \{I_t^{(i)}, y^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^{N}$
\Ensure $\hat{y}^{(i)} \in \{\text{benign}, \text{malignant}\}$ for each subject $i$

\State Initialize parameters $\theta_M$, $\theta_T$, $W_c$, $b_c$
\State Load mammographic and thermographic datasets; perform independent 70 / 15 / 15 percent train, validation and test splits while preserving class balance

\medskip
\Comment{\textit{--- Preprocessing ---}}
\For{each subject $i = 1$ to $N$}
    \State Histogram equalize $I_m^{(i)}$; normalize via Eq.~\eqref{eq:mammo_norm_code}; rescale via Eq.~\eqref{eq:mammo_rescale}
    \State Resize and replicate channels $\Rightarrow I_m''' \in \mathbb{R}^{224\times224\times3}$
    \State Normalize $T^{(i)}$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:temp_norm_code}; apply Gaussian blur; threshold mask via Eq.~\eqref{eq:thermo_mask}
    \State Resize thermogram $\Rightarrow I_t' \in \mathbb{R}^{224\times224\times3}$
\EndFor

\medskip
\Comment{\textit{--- Feature Extraction ---}}
\For{each subject $i = 1$ to $N$}
    \State $f_M^{(i)} \gets \phi_M(I_m'''; \theta_M)$ \hfill $\triangleright$ ResNet50, $f_M \in \mathbb{R}^{2048}$
    \State $f_T^{(i)} \gets \phi_T(I_t'; \theta_T)$ \hfill $\triangleright$ MobileNetV2, $f_T \in \mathbb{R}^{1280}$
\EndFor

\medskip
\Comment{\textit{--- Fusion and Dimensionality Reduction ---}}
\For{each subject $i = 1$ to $N$}
    \State $\mathbf{F}_{\text{fusion}}^{(i)} \gets [f_M^{(i)} \Vert f_T^{(i)}]$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Eq.~\eqref{eq:concat}
    \State $\mathcal{S}^* \gets \text{mRMR}(\mathbf{F}_{\text{fusion}})$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Eq.~\eqref{eq:mrmr_code}
\EndFor

\medskip
\Comment{\textit{--- Training ---}}
\For{each mini batch $\mathcal{B} \subseteq \mathcal{S}^*$}
    \State $\hat{y} \gets \mathrm{Softmax}(W_c \mathbf{F}_{\text{fusion}} + b_c)$ \hfill $\triangleright$ Eq.~\eqref{eq:classifier_code}
    \State Compute $\mathcal{L}$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:loss}
    \State Update $\{\theta_M, \theta_T, W_c\}$ via Adam, $\eta = 10^{-4}$
\EndFor

\medskip
\Comment{\textit{--- Evaluation and Explainability ---}}
\State Evaluate on test set: Accuracy, Precision, Recall, AUC
\State Compute SHAP values $\phi_i$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:shap_code}
\State Visualize SHAP heatmaps and return $\{\hat{y}^{(i)}, \phi_i\}$

\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Experimental Configuration}

All experiments ran in Python on a TensorFlow stack sitting on an NVIDIA GPU. Training used the Adam optimizer at a learning rate of $10^{-4}$ under categorical cross entropy. Batch size 32. Fifty epochs, with early stopping wired in so the model does not keep pushing past its actual generalisation ceiling on the validation split. Evaluation reported accuracy and PR AUC on held out data. As a separate robustness pass, Gaussian noise was injected into the feature vectors at test time to see how the classifier behaved when the inputs were degraded from their clean form.

\subsection{Implementation Details}

The proposed framework was implemented using TensorFlow and Keras in Python. ResNet50 and MobileNetV2 backbones pretrained on ImageNet were fine tuned for mammographic and thermographic feature extraction respectively. During preprocessing, all input images were resized to $224 \times 224$ pixels and normalized prior to feature encoding.

The ResNet50 encoder generated 2048 dimensional mammographic embeddings, while MobileNetV2 produced 1280 dimensional thermographic embeddings. These representations were projected into a shared 128 dimensional latent space using fully connected embedding layers with ReLU activation. Latent alignment was optimized using mean squared error loss during pretraining.

The fused latent representation feeds a lightweight classifier head. A pair of dense layers with dropout in between, then a softmax on the two class output. Training used Adam at learning rate $10^{-4}$ with batch size 32, and both early stopping and validation loss monitoring were kept active throughout training so that the classifier weights being saved were the ones that generalised best rather than the ones that fit the training split tightest.

\subsection{Ablation Study and Comparative Analysis}

To quantify the contribution of individual components of the proposed framework, an ablation study was carried out. It systematically evaluated unimodal baselines, multimodal fusion without latent alignment, and the full proposed model. As shown in Table~\ref{tab:comparison}, multimodal fusion consistently outperforms unimodal approaches. What comes out of the table is exactly the pattern one would predict if the two modalities are carrying complementary information for the binary benign versus malignant call. The mammography only branch reaches 89.4 percent accuracy and PR AUC 0.941. The thermography only branch trails at 86.2 percent accuracy and PR AUC 0.912. Naive concatenation without alignment pushes accuracy to 91.8 percent. Adding the latent alignment step lifts it further to 94.5 percent, with PR AUC 0.991.

Adding the latent alignment step is where the gain shows up cleanly. Fusion without alignment already helps, but the alignment step lifts accuracy another 2.7 percent, and the effect is far from noise. What matters here is that the alignment is pushing the two modalities into a shared coordinate system rather than leaving them as disjoint feature streams which the classifier would otherwise have to reconcile on its own. The robustness sweep tells a similar story. Under injected Gaussian noise, blur and low contrast perturbations, the aligned fusion model degrades by only a few percentage points, unlike the unaligned baseline which drops much more sharply, so the alignment is not just an accuracy trick it also stabilises the representation.

Table~\ref{tab:literature_comparison} positions the proposed model against past work on the same CBIS\textendash DDSM and DMR\textendash IR benchmarks. On CBIS\textendash DDSM alone, the strongest unimodal mammography baselines cluster around 86 to 87 percent accuracy \cite{Shen2019DeepLearningMammo, Ragab2019CBISDDSM}. On DMR\textendash IR alone, thermography deep models attain between 85 and 98 percent depending on architecture and label granularity \cite{Jalloul2024, Alzahrani2025, Roslidar2020}. Existing multimodal designs that combine mammographic and thermographic streams report accuracies of 92 to 94 percent \cite{mostafa2022multi, khan2023hybrid}. The proposed latent fusion pipeline achieves 94.5 percent binary accuracy, which is on par with or above these multimodal baselines and considerably above unimodal mammography. The comparison is indicative rather than head to head, since cohort composition and class definitions vary between studies. Nevertheless, the direction of improvement is consistent: augmenting mammography with thermography, and aligning the two representations, yields measurable benefits.

\subsection{Robustness to Input Perturbations}
To probe robustness we injected zero mean Gaussian noise independently into the thermographic and the mammographic test feature vectors and re evaluated the classifier under this perturbed input regime. The fusion model held its performance. Test accuracy under noise was 93.1 percent against 94.5 percent on the clean test set, a drop of only 1.4 percentage points, and there was no accompanying collapse in the precision recall curve. The fact that each modality stream individually degrades more sharply under the same perturbation but the joint model does not suggests that the two feature branches are absorbing different failure modes and that the alignment step is compensating for them at the latent level rather than at the raw feature level.


\subsection{Explainability via SHAP Analysis}



Fig.~\ref{fig:shap} plots the mean absolute SHAP value across every latent feature dimension. The distribution is sparse. Only a small number of coordinates carry substantial contribution mass while the vast majority remain at or near zero and are effectively silent during inference on the held out test set. This pattern is diagnostic of a classifier that is not treating all 128 latent dimensions equivalently but is instead concentrating its evidence on a compact informative subset, which is desirable both because it renders the downstream SHAP explanations more tractable for a clinician to interpret and because it indicates redundancy in the raw fused representation that a future dimensionality reduction step could exploit.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Multimodal Latent Space Alignment}
The two modalities cannot be fused naively. Their feature spaces are shaped differently, one from a mammographic backbone with 2048 dimensional output and one from a thermographic backbone with 1280 dimensional output, and pushing them straight into a shared classifier would leave the classifier doing implicit alignment work on top of the classification task itself. We instead train two small encoder heads whose only job is to project each modality into a common 128 dimensional latent space. A mean squared error alignment loss between paired thermographic and mammographic embeddings drives the two projections together during a dedicated pretraining phase.

Once the alignment training converges, the two latent distributions look considerably more consistent than they did at initialisation and this is what makes the downstream fusion meaningful. The alignment step is also doing the work of reducing modality specific bias. Without it, the classifier tends to lean disproportionately on whichever modality happens to dominate the joint feature vector by magnitude alone.

\subsection{Summary of Findings}
Bringing the experimental results together the multimodal latent fusion pipeline meets the objectives that were laid out at the start of this study. Thermographic and mammographic information are combined at the latent representation level rather than at raw pixel level and the joint classifier reaches 94.5 percent test accuracy with a PR AUC of 0.991 on the binary benign versus malignant task. Robustness under Gaussian input noise stands at 93.1 percent, only a 1.4 percentage point drop from the clean baseline. Latent space projections through t\textendash SNE and PCA confirm class wise separability, while the SHAP analysis provides per feature attribution over the fused latent representation.

Across the four observations above, the picture that emerges is a coherent one. Latent level multimodal fusion is a workable and reasonably interpretable design for research grade breast cancer analysis. The claim advanced here is one of design and analysis, not of clinical deployment.


\section{Conclusion}
This paper presented a multimodal breast cancer detection pipeline that routes thermographic and mammographic imagery through independent modality specific encoders. Each encoder projects its features into a common 128 dimensional latent space and an alignment objective drives the two projections toward each other in that space so that the corresponding cross modal representations become geometrically comparable at the latent level. The aligned latent embeddings are then concatenated and passed to a binary classifier that discriminates benign from malignant instances.

Empirically the pipeline holds up under evaluation. The joint held out test set drawn from CBIS\textendash DDSM and DMR\textendash IR yields 94.5 percent classification accuracy and a PR AUC of 0.991, and injecting zero mean Gaussian noise into both modality feature streams only reduces accuracy to 93.1 percent. Latent space visualisations under t\textendash SNE and PCA show benign and malignant samples occupying visibly separate regions of the projected space. The pattern across these three observations is consistent. Learned latent codes appear to be organised around disease relevant information rather than modality specific noise or acquisition artefacts.

Beyond the raw performance numbers the paper leaned on SHAP based post hoc interpretability so that individual predictions could be inspected at the level of latent feature contributions. This is not cosmetic. A radiologist reviewing an automated flag will want to see which fused features drove the model toward its call, not only the probability score attached to that call, and the SHAP scaffolding in this pipeline is what makes that kind of per prediction inspection possible in practice.

The takeaway of the study is that latent level multimodal fusion is a workable strategy for automated breast cancer analysis. Robustness under input noise held up in the ablation. There is a measurable performance gap over the unimodal baseline. And per prediction interpretability through SHAP is available at inference time rather than being retrofitted after the fact. Three follow on directions look worth pursuing. Larger, clinically more diverse cohorts come first, ideally with multi site acquisition variability and biopsy confirmed labels, since the current numbers rest on two mid sized public corpora. Explicit cross modal attention between the two backbones is the next step architecturally, so that mammographic and thermographic evidence can weight each other adaptively rather than through the flat concatenation used here. Longitudinal and near real time diagnostic use is the third direction, where the classifier is expected to update its state as fresh scans arrive for the same patient over time rather than making one shot predictions on a static image.
